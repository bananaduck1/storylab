The morning I found the rejection letter from the summer research program, I locked my bedroom door and disassembled my entire desk.

Not out of anger—out of necessity. The desk had a wobbly leg I'd been ignoring for months, shimming it with folded paper. That morning, the shim fell out and the whole surface tilted, sending the letter sliding to the floor like it wanted to escape too. So I grabbed a screwdriver and pulled every joint apart.

I laid thirty-seven screws on my carpet in a grid. I sanded the stripped holes with a nail file. I borrowed wood glue from Mr. Farhadi next door, who asked no questions but handed me a clamp and said, "Overnight." I rebuilt the desk that evening, tighter than the factory version, and placed the rejection letter in the center drawer.

My mother asked why I was so calm. I told her the desk taught me something the letter couldn't: that when a structure fails, you don't throw it away. You find the joint that slipped and fix it there. The research proposal had failed at the methodology joint—I'd tried to design an experiment without understanding the controls. So I emailed Dr. Priya Chen, whose paper I'd cited, and asked her to show me what I'd gotten wrong.

She wrote back in three hours. Her first line was: "Most students don't ask that question."

Six months later I resubmitted, not to the same program but to one where the methodology mattered more than the institution's name. I got in. But more importantly, I stopped seeing rejection as a wall and started seeing it as a wobbly leg—a specific failure at a specific joint, fixable if you're willing to take everything apart first.
