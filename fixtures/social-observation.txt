Every Saturday at 6 a.m., Mrs. Kovac opens the side door of the Hungarian bakery on Corbin Avenue and sets a paper bag on the step. Inside are two day-old kifli and a napkin folded into a triangle.

The bag is for Dante, who sleeps behind the laundromat next door. He never enters the bakery. She never invites him in. They have been performing this exchange for at least three years, because I've watched it from my bedroom window since eighth grade, when insomnia turned me into an accidental anthropologist of my own block.

I notice what people do when they think no one is keeping score. Mr. Huang tapes his "CLOSED" sign crooked every Sunday—always the same corner lifted—and I've decided it's deliberate, his quiet protest against perfection. The Delgado twins race their bikes to the stop sign and back exactly four times before school, never three, never five, as if the count itself holds the morning together.

These are not important observations. No one will write a paper about crooked signs or kifli bags. But watching my block taught me something I couldn't learn from any textbook: that people build rituals to say things they can't say out loud. Mrs. Kovac's bag says "I see you." Mr. Huang's sign says "I'm still here on my own terms." The twins' laps say "Today is real; we counted it."

I want to study sociology not because I read about it but because I've practiced it from a second-floor window for four years. I already know the first rule: the data that matters most is the data people produce when they believe no one is recording it.
