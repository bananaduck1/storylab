I used to think the law was something to fear.

Growing up in a neighborhood where police sirens scored every evening, I associated courtrooms with loss—my uncle's custody hearing, my neighbor's eviction appeal, the day my mother sat rigid in a plastic chair waiting to hear if our lease would hold. The law was a hammer, and we were always the nail.

That changed the summer I turned fifteen. My high school offered a mock-trial elective, and I signed up mostly to fill a gap in my schedule. But standing behind the podium for the first time, reading an opening statement I had written myself, I felt something unexpected: the room leaned in. The words I chose made people listen, not because I was loud but because the argument had structure, each sentence building on the last like bricks in a wall that suddenly looked intentional.

What my fists could never have solved that night next to the movie theater—when a stranger shoved my younger brother and every muscle in me screamed to swing—my voice could one day settle in a courtroom. I don't mean that poetically. I mean I stood there shaking, fists balled, and chose instead to memorize the man's face, his jacket, the license plate half-visible under a streetlight, because I had learned that evidence outlasts anger.

I now believe the law is not a hammer. It is a language, and like any language it belongs to whoever learns to speak it clearly. That belief costs something: it means I cannot afford the satisfying shortcut of outrage. It means sitting with my uncle after another continuance and explaining, again, why patience is strategy, not weakness—even when I'm not sure I believe it myself. It means choosing the long way every single time and trusting that precision will matter more than volume.

I don't know yet if I'm right. But I know that the fifteen-year-old who memorized a license plate instead of throwing a punch was already practicing law, even if no one told him so.
