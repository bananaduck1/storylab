Introduction

Hi, my name is Sam.
In 2020, I was admitted to all four schools I applied to: Yale, Harvard, Stanford, and Princeton.

I chose to attend Yale, where I studied the humanities, graduated magna cum laude and Phi Beta Kappa with Distinction in my major, and won university writing prizes. I also worked as an interviewer with the Yale Admissions Office, helping admissions officers decide who to admit to one of the most selective institutions in the world.

Since then, I’ve helped students earn admission to their top-choice schools by applying the same philosophy I used in my own process: vulnerability over accomplishment, reflection over résumé-building, and writing that feels unmistakably human.

My Approach (Value Add)

Many families come to me after receiving advice that sounds polished but interchangeable. Much of today’s admissions guidance is built around formulas—what to emphasize, how to frame achievements, how to sound “impressive.”

That approach matters even less now.

We’re in the era of AI. As more applicants use tools like ChatGPT to draft essays, writing that feels generic—or overly polished—stands out immediately, and not in a good way. In this environment, originality and human specificity are no longer nice-to-haves; they are often the difference between an essay that fades and one that lingers.

Admissions officers are not looking to be impressed. They are looking to remember someone. That kind of writing can’t be automated. It has to be read closely, questioned carefully, and revised with intention.

My feedback centers on humanizing students on the page—helping them sound like real people with interior lives, not like well-edited profiles. I work closely with a small number of students, getting to know them as people rather than profiles. I read essays the way an admissions officer does: asking whether the voice feels honest, whether the thinking feels lived-in, and whether I would want to meet this person in real life. 

I don’t run a volume operation; I take on select clients so I can read closely, think carefully, and help students tell the truest version of their story—one that cuts through the noise because it isn’t trying to.

Pre-12th Grade Advising

I strongly recommend working together before senior year—sometimes as early as eighth grade.

By 12th grade, many students are already locked into narratives that don’t fully reflect who they are. Earlier guidance allows students to develop their voice over time, rather than trying to manufacture depth at the last minute.

At this stage, I work with students not only on long-term strategy, but also on writing itself. This includes helping students craft essays for competitions, prizes, and selective programs—writing that develops their thinking, sharpens their voice, and often strengthens their future applications as a byproduct.

The goal is not résumé-padding. It’s to help students, well before the admissions process begins, learn how to write with clarity, depth, and originality, all skills that will take them far in life beyond college.

Do you work directly with students or mostly with parents?

I work directly with students, but I loop parents in as appropriate. Transparency and alignment matter, especially in a process that affects the whole family.

How do you think about AI tools like ChatGPT?

AI can be useful for surface-level help—word choice, clarity, organization. But strategically, it’s limited. When everyone uses the same tools, everyone starts to sound the same. Essays succeed when they feel lived-in and personal, not optimized.

Do you guarantee outcomes?

No. No one can guarantee admissions results. What I do guarantee is thoughtful, well-written essays that help students find and articulate their voice—something that remains valuable regardless of outcome.

When is the right time to start working together?

As early as possible. Earlier work allows students to develop their voice over time instead of trying to manufacture depth during senior year.

How hands-on are you with essays?

It depends on the package. I’m meticulous with feedback—editing carefully while preserving the student’s voice. Think of it as editing with a surgeon’s knife, not a sledgehammer.
